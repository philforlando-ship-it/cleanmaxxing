# Who is Mister P?

## Start with me.

I'm Phil. I'm the founder of Cleanmaxxing. Mister P is the voice of the product — direct, a little dry, willing to tell you when something isn't worth your time — and he exists because of a pattern in my own life I eventually got tired of walking around inside.

This is the honest version of why I built this.

## The peak

When I was in good shape — lean, tan, training — my face changed. My jawline came out, my cheekbones emerged, the whole thing sharpened. I've got the genetics where working out makes my arms grow faster than the rest of me, and I'm tall, which is a multiplier: tall and thin reads as *tall plus thin*, and tall and fat reads as nothing at all. At peak, I went from something like a 6 to an 8. With hair, maybe 8.75. People I'd gone to high school with looked at me a few years later like they couldn't quite reconcile it with who they remembered — almost floored at how much had changed.

And the attention was good. Success built on success, confidence built on confidence. People wanted to be around me more. I noticed.

I also abused it.

For a stretch of time, the peak made me more of a prick. The ego inflation was real. I mistook a three-year genetic window for a personality. If I'd been grounded then the way I am now — a wife I've been married to for nine years, a kid, another one about to arrive — I would have handled it better and used it for something more than a scoreboard.

I didn't. And then the window closed, the way everyone's window eventually does.

## How I got there

I grew up with a birthmark on the right side of my forehead, about the size of a dime. Kids started pointing it out in the fourth or fifth grade. In eighth grade I arranged to have it removed — the kind of procedure that would leave a small scar, which I was fine with, and which would have healed in time for fall soccer. A week before the appointment the hospital called and cancelled because the doctor wasn't going to be in. I never rescheduled. It faded on its own over the years, but the version of me who grew up around it was already shaped.

Then acne through most of middle school and high school. Then the low baseline of confidence that goes with not being told you're awesome very much at home — I don't think my dad knew how to do that part, and I don't hold it against him, but the effect was real. I was also socially immature, which made every normal teenage confidence-building moment harder to come by. The feedback loops that build easy confidence in other kids didn't quite close for me.

Junior and senior year of high school, I started picking my own body apart. Too much body hair. The way my belly button looked when I took my shirt off. How pale I was. The hairs on my shoulders. None of it was remarkable from the outside, and all of it was constant from the inside.

Then the hair.

## The hair

Second semester of freshman year of college, I noticed I was receding. People at the grocery store I worked at part-time started making fun of the reflection off the back of my head. The vertex was going too. Sophomore year I started on Propecia — eighty-five dollars a month at the time, which was a meaningful chunk of what I was making. Rogaine foam made my scalp itch. I wasn't great at taking Propecia consistently because of the cost, and because I wasn't sure if it was working, since I was still watching hair come out in the shower. So I eventually stopped — and that's when I found out how much it had been helping, because without it the hair came off fast.

And the jokes didn't stop when I left the grocery store. My first real job out of college was at a financial institution, and the people I worked with there picked up where the college kids had left off. A different room, a different dress code, the same reflection off the back of my head.

From about 26 until 35, I used X Fusion hair fibers every day. Caked them on. Pillows at girls' houses would turn dark the first morning. The cloth headliner of every Uber I sat in picked up black spots where my head touched it. I couldn't go in the ocean without it running. The wind on the walk through downtown Boston to work would redistribute the fibers until I looked like a bird. In two-dimensional photos I looked fine — great, even — but on video, after a few seconds, the density gave itself away.

Five years after I should have stopped is when I actually stopped.

## Where I am now

I've got a body that puts on muscle easily. It also puts on fat easily. For the last ten or fifteen years I've been on and off diets and on and off workout regimens, and the "off" part was almost always an injury or Halloween or a bad week of eating that rolled into Thanksgiving. Then whatever I'd built would unravel and I'd start over in January.

I missed most of my thirties on the anti-aging side. For the last three or four years I've been putting on a moisturizer, probably the wrong one, inconsistently, without knowing that retinoids and SPF and cleansers were supposed to be part of it. I took a multivitamin for a long time before I learned it was basically a waste. I started fish oil again a month ago because two friends were on it. I'm taking creatine gummies for the first time in fifteen years. I'm forty-one.

## The thing I figured out on the way back

There are two things that look similar from a distance and aren't.

One is *looking better.* Looking better gets you attention, compliments, and a better first impression. It's real, it's worth having, and it's also completely external. If your sense of self is built on top of it, then the day your body composition slips, or your hair thins, or you turn forty and your face changes — your sense of self slips too. That's the trap I walked into, and walked back out of the hard way.

The other is *working on yourself.* Showing up when you don't feel like it. Learning what actually works. Building a system that holds when a holiday or an injury or a bad week hits. Getting better at something difficult over a long period of time. That one is internal, it's durable, and the research actually backs this up — the psychology literature on self-esteem (Jennifer Crocker on contingencies of self-worth, Nathaniel Branden on the six pillars) is pretty clear that self-esteem built on external metrics like appearance and approval is fragile, and self-esteem built on internal practices like agency, competence, integrity, and purpose is not.

Cleanmaxxing is built to separate those two things.

Work on how you look — but don't tie your worth as a human being to where you rank. Use the system to build real competence and real agency, and let the better appearance be a side effect of the work, not the scoreboard you live on. That's why daily check-ins are about goals, not global self-worth. Why confidence is tracked weekly and across specific contexts instead of one overall rating. Why Mister P will tell you when something isn't worth fixing. Why the homepage explicitly names three other paths to self-confidence we don't own. Why we don't do tier lists, "high-value male" language, or any of the other framings that turn appearance into a hierarchy of worth.

## Who Mister P actually is

Mister P is the voice I built into the product because I wanted the kind of assistant I wish I'd had at thirty-five. Direct. A little dry. Grounded in a 100–200k+ word corpus of opinionated content about what actually moves the needle, and willing to cite the source on every answer.

He won't sell you supplements. He won't link to vendors. He won't prescribe a cycle. He'll discuss TRT and peptides and GLP-1s as education — including the mechanisms, the risks, and the honest tradeoffs — and when a clinical question comes up he'll tell you to take it to your physician. Cleanmaxxing is not a medical service. We don't interpret lab results and we don't pretend to.

Sometimes the answer Mister P gives you is *this one isn't worth fixing — focus on something else.*

That isn't a content policy. That's the advice I needed and didn't get, written into the product on purpose.

## What this is for

If you're somewhere on the curve I was on — whether that's the peaked-and-watched-it-close version, or the balding version, or the on-and-off-again version, or the missed-the-anti-aging-window version, or all four — this is built for you. Not to turn you into a high-value male. Not to move you up a ranking. Not to optimize you perfectly for a season. To help you become the kind of person for whom looking and performing well is simply how you live — consistently, sustainably, and without burning yourself out in the process.

That's what I'm trying to build. It's the version I would have paid for at twenty-six, at thirty-five, and at forty-one.

— Phil
